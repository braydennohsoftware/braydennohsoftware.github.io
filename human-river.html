1. Introduction

Throughout history, meandering rivers have acted as spatial attractors for human civilization. Rivers provide food and transportation, minimizing the thermodynamic work required for resource extraction and navigation. Cities like Cairo, London, and Kolkata all grew around river bends.

But rivers are also destroyers. Through a process called avulsion, a river can suddenly shift course and wipe out settlements in its path. In this sense, a river functions as an unpredictable attractor — one that can create and destroy, analogous to a black hole.

Human-River is a computational model that couples these two forces — the pull of the river and the resilience of human settlements — into a single dynamical system. It is the first model of its kind to treat human and geomorphic evolution as a coupled process.

2. The Problem

Human settlements near rivers are shaped by three competing forces:

  1. River attraction. Settlements want to be close to the river for resources and transportation.
  2. Economies of scale. Settlements want to cluster together to share public goods and reduce transaction costs (the bid-rent model from urban economics).
  3. Spatial inertia. A settlement will not abandon existing functional infrastructure the moment a marginally better location appears. Relocation only happens when the environmental stress gradient exceeds a critical resistance threshold.

The question is: what happens when you let these three rules play out on a grid while a river meanders through it? What stable patterns emerge? When do settlements jump across the river, go extinct, or sprawl outward?

3. Setting Up the Model

3.1 The Lattice

The model runs on a two-dimensional grid (lattice). Each cell on the grid is one of three things:

  • River cell — R(x, t) = 1
  • Settlement cell — V(x, t) = 1 on a floodplain cell
  • Empty floodplain — R = 0 and V = 0

One key rule: a settlement cell can never occupy the same cell as a river cell. If the river moves into a settlement, that settlement is destroyed.

3.2 Conservation Laws

The model assumes population is conserved — the total number of settlement cells N stays constant over time. If a settlement is destroyed by the river, an equivalent settlement must appear elsewhere on the floodplain. Think of it as displaced people relocating rather than disappearing.

3.3 The Utility Field

Each floodplain cell has a utility score that determines how attractive it is for settlement. The utility is defined as: 

U(x, t) = -w_d * D(x, t) + w_c * C(x, t)

Where:

  • D(x, t) is the distance to the nearest river cell — farther from the river means lower utility (less access to resources).
  • C(x, t) is the local clustering score — how many of the 8 neighboring cells (Moore neighborhood) are also settlements. More neighbors means higher utility (economies of scale).
  • w_d and w_c are tunable coupling constants that control the relative strength of each force.

River cells themselves have a utility of negative infinity — settlements can never occupy them.

4. How the Simulation Runs

4.1 Geomorphic Migration

As the river meanders across the grid, it can invade floodplain cells that contain settlements. When this happens:

  1. The settlement cell is destroyed (set to 0).
  2. To conserve mass, a new settlement cell is placed at the empty floodplain cell with the highest utility.

This models the real-world process of communities being displaced by flooding and relocating to the next best available location.

4.2 Gradient-Driven Migration

Even when the river isn't destroying anything, settlements continuously try to improve their position. At each timestep, the model:

  1. Finds the least happy settlement cell (lowest utility).
  2. Finds the best available empty floodplain cell (highest utility).
  3. Swaps them — but only if the utility gain exceeds a friction threshold μ.

The friction parameter μ represents spatial inertia — the sunk costs of abandoning existing infrastructure. A high μ means settlements are stubborn and won't move unless conditions are dramatically better elsewhere. A low μ means settlements are highly mobile.

The system keeps swapping until no further beneficial moves exist, reaching a stable configuration for that timestep. Then the river advances and the process repeats.

5. What Emerges

By varying the parameters (w_d, w_c, μ) and the dynamics (linear vs. non-linear, deterministic vs. stochastic, causally directed vs. coupled), the model explores:

  • Stable regimes — settlements that reach a steady-state configuration relative to the river.
  • Transient regimes — settlements in constant flux, never reaching equilibrium.
  • Threshold behavior — sudden phase transitions such as settlements jumping across the river, urban sprawl, or settlement extinction.

The model investigates which properties remain invariant across different dynamic regimes — and which ones break down.

6. Applications

This model has implications for:

  • Urban planning — understanding how cities naturally organize around shifting waterways.
  • Disaster risk mitigation — predicting which settlements are most vulnerable to river migration.
  • Theoretical bounds on forecasting — setting upper limits on how accurately we can predict coupled human-geomorphic systems.
